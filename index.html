What is the Ride Guide?

The Ride Guide is a short, concise way of describing ride characteristics. There are 3 primary categories (distance/hilliness/SSSS) to help people better understand what to expect from a given ride.

There are also 3 supplemental categories (food/water/# stops) that we encourage ride leaders to include as well.

More detail on each category can be found throughout this document.

Distance

Distance is mostly self-explanatory: how long is the ride? But more importantly, how do we anticipate that the length will impact the overall difficulty of the ride? For the sake of offering some concrete numbers we are going to say that a 1 on the scale is 10 miles or less and a 5 is a century ride (100 miles or more).

Of course, like all things cycling, much of this is subjective and a person’s past performance will likely dictate how they view the length of a ride. We would encourage ride leaders to err on the side of caution and give a higher rating if there is any doubt as to where on the scale a ride falls.

Hilliness

Let’s be real, most people tend to suffer on the climbs. There is a reason why it’s KOM: “King Of the Mountain” and not “King Of the Flats” (though there are certainly flat KOMs on Strava). We want to give people an idea of what the route will “feel” like when it comes to elevation gain.

But it is important to stress that this is not an exact science. Some rides have a lot of elevation gain with rolling hills. Some rides have moderate amounts of elevation gain but really steep climbs. We’re trying to provide general guidance.

In general we would ask ride leaders to weigh steeper climbs more heavily than climbs that don’t pitch up as much. The following table is a good starting point though when considering the average rider.

Self Sufficiency / Suffer Scale

This is the information people are generally looking for when they ask questions like: “what is the average speed of the ride?” Most people who ask questions about “pace” and “average speed” really want to know one of two things: 

  1. Am I going to be left in the dust?
  2. Is this ride going to be painfully slow? 

Our attempt to answer this question involves creating several categories. These categories include general guidance for riders of different abilities. The ride leader may give additional guidance on their particular ride if needed. Assume that what the ride leader says takes precedence over what the SS/SS category says.

Leasure

  • Don’t bother putting your kit on
  • Literally anyone with a working bike and no limiting health problems should be able to do this
  • Likely to have lots of stops both planned and otherwise
  • Absolutely no drop
  • Fast riders will likely find this tedious
  • Almost no FOB! Rides will fall under this category

Social

  • Kit is a matter of preference though you won’t need any aero advantage to keep up
  • We keep it rolling but almost everyone should be able to hold a conversation while riding
  • There may be several planned stops but we try to limit unplanned stops
  • Fast riders will likely find this tedious
  • Absolutely no drop

Presto

Italian for “quick”

  • You probably want to get kitted up and you probably want to leave the paniers at home
  • We focus on the ride, we have clearly defined stops and will not deviate unless there is an emergency
  • Generally speaking the ride leader will not stop to regroup very often (their discretion)
  • That means there is a possibility you will be dropped
  • You must know the route and have a backup plan if you get dropped
  • It may be a good idea for the group to split up to accommodate riders of different skill/fitness levels
  • You may find this grueling if you’ve not done rides in excess of 30 miles very often (or ever)
  • Moderately experienced riders may find this to be both a challenge and somewhat difficult
  • Strong/Experienced riders will not find this difficult but still somewhat of a challenge
  • Particularly strong riders may still find this tedious

Puncher

  • This one is primarily for the roadies
  • Maximizing moving time is paramount, stops are limited to only what we need to be able to refuel and keep going
  • You will be dropped if you can’t keep up
  • You must know the route
  • Moderately experienced riders will find this to be difficult
  • Strong/Experienced riders will find this to be somewhat difficult

GC

Stands for general classification and Garrett County, how convenient!

  • This is going to be hard for literally everyone, no matter how strong you are
  • Some people are just not going to be able to complete this kind of ride
  • You will be dropped if you can’t keep up
  • If you don’t get the Garrett County reference look for GCGF 2019 on my strava profile
